About This Project

The Problem Space: Play is often viewed as a privilege, and yet it is a necessity to ensure proper early childhood development — fostering cognitive, social-emotional, and regulatory skills in children (Luebbe, 2026; Nwokah et al., 2013). Access to play materials can significantly enhance a child's problem-solving, communication, and personal-social competencies (Wu et al., 2019). However, in under-developed communities, resource scarcity means these materials are often the first items sacrificed. This shortage contributes to developmental delays that risk perpetuating the cycle of poverty (Wu et al., 2019). At the same time, commercial toys have evolved to promote an unhealthy culture of consumerism, reducing toys to a form of social currency (Patico, 2011) — creating an "economy of dignity" where children without popular toys risk social exclusion.

Target Audience: Children and families in under-developed communities who lack the purchasing power for commercial toys but require access to essential developmental tools.

The Framework: Using Ezio Manzini's concept of Diffuse Design, this project addresses market failure by empowering parents — as non-experts — to use resources they already own to solve the problem of play (Manzini, 2015). Rather than relying on expert-designed commercial products, parents and children create play environments from household items, such as kitchen utensils or discarded materials (Weldon, 2012). This builds a more distributed and resilient play ecosystem that removes the financial burden of purchasing new products (Nwokah et al., 2013), while shifting the symbol of parental care from purchasing toys toward guided interaction and play (Zosh et al., 2022).
